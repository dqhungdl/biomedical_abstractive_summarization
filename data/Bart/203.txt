Acute pancreatitis occurs suddenly and usually goes away in a few days with treatment. Chronic pancreatitis does not heal or improve. It gets worse over time and leads to permanent damage. Common symptoms are severe pain in the upper abdomen, nausea, and vomiting. Treatment for acute or chronic pancreatitis may include a hospital stay to treat dehydration with intravenous (IV) fluids and, if you can swallow them, fluids by mouth pain medicine, and antibiotics by mouth or through an IV if you have an infection in your pancreas. A low-fat diet, or nutrition by feeding tube or IV ifyou can't eat. Inserting a tube through the nose or mouth to remove the contents of the stomach (nasogastric suctioning) may sometimes be done. If your pancreatitis is more severe, your treatment may also include:Surgery. Your doctor may recommend surgery to remove your gallbladder, called cholecystectomy. If you have severe pancreatitis, your doctor may advise delaying surgery to first treat complications. Stopping food or fluid by mouth to limit the activity of the pancrea. Starting an oral diet and slowly starting an Oral diet. Getting enough vitamins and calcium in the diet. Limiting caffeine. The health care provider may prescribe pancreatic enzymes. The enzymes will help you digest food better, gain weight and reduce diarrhea. A nutritionist can help you create a diet that includes: Drinking plenty of liquids Limiting fats Eating small, frequent meals (this helps reduce digestive symptoms) Getting enough minerals and supplements such as calcium or as extra supplements. Avoid smoking and drinking alcoholic beverages, even if your Pancreatitis is mild.
