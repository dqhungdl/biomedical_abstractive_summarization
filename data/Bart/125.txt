Scientists are exploring how lupus develops and how therapies affect the course of the disease. They are also exploring drugs to prevent or delay kidney failure. Experts are also collaborating to develop registries and working groups, such as: The Lupus Family Registry and Repository, which gathers medical information as well as blood and tissue samples from patients and their relatives. Eat a healthy well-balanced diet rich in fruits, vegetables, and whole grains. Exercise to help keep your body strong; however, talk to your doctor before starting an exercise program. This will help lower your risk for heart disease that can be a complication ofLupus. Close monitoring during pregnancy is essential, especially if you have low platelets, antiphospholipid antibodies, anti-SSA/Ro antibodies, high blood pressure, lung or heart problems, or kidney disease. Birth control pills do not increase the risk for severe flares among women with l upus, but estrogen-containing pills are not recommended for women with antiph phosphate antibodies. If you start to plan a pregnancy, talk with your doctor so you can be as healthy as possible before becoming pregnant. Most people with mild disease or who are in remission can usually participate in the same life activities as they did before they were diagnosed with lupu disease. The disease can affect the skin, joints, heart, lung, kidneys, circulating blood cells, and brain. It is a chronic (long-lasting) autoimmune disease that affects many parts of the body.
